❤️ About Heart Disease

Heart disease refers to any condition affecting the heart. There are many types, some of which are preventable. Unlike cardiovascular disease, which includes problems with the entire circulatory system, heart disease affects only the heart.

According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), heart disease is the leading cause of death in the United States. Around 1 in 4 deaths in the U.S. occur due to heart disease, and it affects all genders as well as all racial and ethnic groups.

🔬 Types of Heart Disease

There are several different types of heart disease, and they affect the heart in different ways.

Coronary Artery Disease

The most common type of heart disease. It develops when the arteries that supply blood to the heart become clogged with plaque, causing them to harden and narrow. This reduces blood supply and can lead to heart failure and arrhythmias.

Congenital Heart Defects

A person with a congenital heart defect is born with a heart problem. Types include:

  • Abnormal heart valves that don't open properly or leak blood
  • Septal defects — a hole in the wall between heart chambers
  • Atresia — one of the heart valves is missing

Arrhythmia

An irregular heartbeat caused when electrical impulses don't work properly. Types include tachycardia (fast), bradycardia (slow), premature contractions, and atrial fibrillation.

Dilated Cardiomyopathy

The heart chambers become dilated, stretching and thinning the heart muscle. Most commonly caused by prior heart attacks, arrhythmias, and toxins. Usually affects people aged 20–60 years.

Myocardial Infarction (Heart Attack)

An interruption of blood flow to the heart that can damage or destroy part of the heart muscle. Most commonly caused by plaque or a blood clot in a coronary artery.

Heart Failure

When the heart is still working but not as well as it should. Can result from untreated coronary artery disease, high blood pressure, or arrhythmias. Early treatment is crucial.

Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy

A genetic condition where the heart muscle walls thicken, making it harder to pump blood. It is the main cause of cardiac death among athletes and people under 35 years old.

Mitral Valve Regurgitation

The mitral valve doesn't close tightly, allowing blood to flow back into the heart. Over time, the heart can become enlarged and heart failure can result.

Aortic Stenosis

The aortic valve opening is too narrow, restricting blood flow from the left ventricle to the aorta. Can be present at birth or develop due to calcium deposits or scarring.

🩺 Symptoms

Heart disease symptoms depend on what type of heart disease you have.

Blood Vessel Symptoms

  • Chest pain, tightness, pressure and discomfort (angina)
  • Shortness of breath
  • Pain, numbness, weakness or coldness in legs or arms
  • Pain in the neck, jaw, throat, upper abdomen or back

Arrhythmia Symptoms

  • Fluttering in your chest
  • Racing heartbeat (tachycardia) or slow heartbeat (bradycardia)
  • Chest pain or discomfort
  • Shortness of breath, lightheadedness, dizziness
  • Fainting (syncope) or near fainting

Cardiomyopathy Symptoms

  • Breathlessness with activity or at rest
  • Swelling of the legs, ankles and feet
  • Fatigue and irregular heartbeats
  • Dizziness, lightheadedness and fainting

Heart Infection Symptoms

  • Fever and shortness of breath
  • Weakness or fatigue
  • Swelling in legs or abdomen
  • Changes in heart rhythm, dry or persistent cough
  • Skin rashes or unusual spots
🚨
When to See a Doctor Immediately
  • Chest pain or severe chest pressure
  • Sudden shortness of breath
  • Fainting or loss of consciousness

⚠️ Risk Factors

Understanding your risk factors is the first step in prevention.

Age

Older age increases risk of narrowed arteries and weakened heart muscle.

Sex

Men are generally at greater risk. Women's risk increases after menopause.

Family History

Family history of heart disease significantly increases your personal risk.

Smoking

Nicotine tightens blood vessels; carbon monoxide damages their lining.

Poor Diet

High fat, salt, sugar and cholesterol diet contributes to heart disease.

High Blood Pressure

Uncontrolled hypertension hardens and thickens your arteries over time.

High Cholesterol

High cholesterol increases the risk of plaque formation and atherosclerosis.

Diabetes

Diabetes increases heart disease risk; both share obesity and BP risk factors.

Obesity

Excess weight worsens nearly all other heart disease risk factors.

Physical Inactivity

Lack of exercise is associated with many forms of heart disease.

Stress

Unrelieved stress can damage arteries and worsen other risk factors.

Poor Dental Health

Unhealthy gums can allow germs to enter bloodstream and reach the heart.

🛡️ Prevention

Certain types of heart disease can't be prevented, but lifestyle changes can significantly reduce your risk.
